DynaClear: Automatic video object removal and inpainting framework

Qualitative inpainting and mask_vis on DAVIS, with quantitative summaries across 8 sequences and 5 methods including mask fusion.

Abstract

Video object removal requires temporally stable segmentation of a target region together with plausible inpainting of the occluded background. Strong video segmenters typically assume explicit user interaction on the first frame, while geometry-aware motion cues can propose foreground-like regions automatically but often yield fragmented masks or weak alignment with object boundaries when a single salient instance must be removed.

DynaClear is a fully automatic pipeline that couples VGGT4D dynamic-region cues with dual-tracker mask propagation (XMem + SAM3), consensus-based vote fusion, and DiffuEraser diffusion-based inpainting. VGGT4D produces per-frame dynamic masks automatically consolidated into an initialization; XMem and SAM3 independently propagate it using two-stage anchoring; their outputs are fused via per-pixel intersection with area-ratio fallback; DiffuEraser then inpaints conditioned on the refined mask sequence. Compared with feeding raw VGGT-style dynamic masks directly to the inpainter, dual-tracker fusion substantially improves boundary precision (\(F_m\), \(F_r\)) and mask score; compared with interactive-only setups, the pipeline derives prompts from motion priors without manual clicks or boxes.

Method

Problem formulation

Given a video \(\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T}\), our goal is to produce an edited video \(\{\hat{I}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}\) where a target region \(M_t\) is removed at each frame. Following the formulation in modern detection and segmentation, the system can be written as

\[ \{M_t\}_{t=1}^{T} = \mathcal{S}(\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T}),\quad \{\hat{I}_t\}_{t=1}^{T} = \mathcal{F}(\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T}, \{M_t\}_{t=1}^{T}), \]

where \(\mathcal{S}\) is an automatic segmentation module and \(\mathcal{F}\) is an inpainting model.

Pipeline overview

Figure 1 summarizes the full automatic route: (1) VGGT4D yields per-frame dynamic masks as a motion and geometry prior; (2) that prior is fused into a single initialization mask that prompts XMem and SAM3 for dual-tracker video object segmentation (VOS), giving two complementary mask sequences; (3) a vote fusion stage combines outputs via intersection with area-ratio fallback; (4) DiffuEraser or ProPainter performs inpainting on the fused mask sequence. The design keeps automatic "what moves" discovery from motion cues while leveraging tracker diversity for boundary fidelity and temporal stability.

Figure 1 — Full automatic removal pipeline (VGGT4D + XMem/SAM3 dual-tracker VOS + vote fusion + inpainting).

Dynamic mask extraction with VGGT4D

We first apply VGGT4D to obtain a coarse estimate of dynamic regions. By processing frames in temporal chunks and applying motion thresholds, we produce per-frame binary or soft masks \(\{\tilde{M}_t\}\) that highlight potential foreground candidates based on 4D geometry cues.

From dynamic masks to an initialization mask

Because promptable VOS models like SAM3 require an initial object specification, we automatically fuse multiple VGGT4D outputs into a cohesive initialization mask \(M^{\mathrm{init}}\). This step converts noisy, multi-object motion priors into a single, trackable foreground hypothesis.

Mask propagation and refinement with SAM 3

Given the automatically constructed initialization mask \(M^{\mathrm{init}}\), we employ SAM3 as a promptable VOS module to propagate an instance-level mask sequence over the entire video. Concretely, we insert \(M^{\mathrm{init}}\) as an indexed mask prompt at its corresponding frame and run SAM3 tracking both forward and backward in time, producing a first-pass prediction \(\{\hat{M}^{(1)}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}\).

Two-stage automatic anchoring. In the absence of human prompts, promptable VOS propagation can occasionally drift to background regions, yielding near-full-frame false positives or stripe-like bands under large camera motion. To stabilize the foreground while remaining fully automatic, we introduce a two-stage anchoring strategy.

Anchor selection (automatic). From the stage-1 predictions, we select an anchor frame index \(a\) by sampling \(k\) evenly spaced candidate frames (with \(k\) clamped to \([5,7]\) when possible). For each candidate, we measure the stage-1 foreground area fraction. We discard candidates that are (i) near full-screen, (ii) too small to be reliable, or (iii) dominated by thin full-width/full-height stripes. Among the remaining candidates, we choose the anchor with the smallest foreground area (ties broken toward later frames).

Stage-2 re-initialization + prefix merge. We then re-run SAM3 using the stage-1 predicted mask at the anchor frame as a new prompt, producing \(\{\hat{M}^{(2)}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}\). Finally, we form the output mask sequence by a simple prefix replacement:

\[ M_t = \begin{cases} \hat{M}^{(2)}_t, & t \le a,\\ \hat{M}^{(1)}_t, & t > a. \end{cases} \]

This design acts as an automatic re-lock of the tracked object at a reliable time while preventing over-correction from propagating through the entire sequence.

Mask propagation with XMem

In parallel to SAM3, we also propagate the initialization mask using XMem, a memory-network-based tracker. Starting from the initialization frame, XMem performs a forward pass followed by a reverse pass. An anchor frame is selected by maximum overlap between forward and reverse masks; objects are validated via an overlap threshold, then re-propagated to stabilize prefix frames. XMem's pixel-level feature matching yields sharper boundaries than SAM3, but may introduce spurious regions in cluttered backgrounds.

Post-processing and alignment

Following propagation, we convert the SAM3 outputs into standardized binary mask sequences and align them with the original video frames. This step prepares the exact frame–mask pairs required by the downstream inpainting stage and generates visual overlays for qualitative evaluation.

Diffusion-based video inpainting with DiffuEraser

Finally, we inpaint the masked regions using DiffuEraser. This module consumes the original frames \(\{I_t\}\) and refined masks \(\{M_t\}\) to synthesize the final edited video \(\{\hat{I}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}\). The quality of this generative process relies heavily on diffusion priors (e.g., latent diffusion) and conditional control mechanisms.

Mask fusion: XMem + SAM3 vote

To further improve mask robustness across diverse sequences, we introduce a mask fusion strategy. Given the tracked masks from two independent propagation pipelines (XMem and SAM3), we apply per-frame intersection voting:

\[ M^{\mathrm{fuse}}_t = M^{\mathrm{xmem}}_t \cap M^{\mathrm{sam3}}_t, \]

where intersection is taken only when the area ratio between the two masks is below a threshold (default 1.5), thereby suppressing contradictory predictions. The fused mask sequence can be inpainted with either ProPainter (vggt4d_xmem_sam3_vote) or DiffuEraser (vggt4d_xmem_sam3_vote_diffueraser). Empirically, vote fusion tends to improve boundary precision (\(F_m\), \(F_r\)) while preserving region similarity (\(J_m\)), as reported in the quantitative table.

Automatic baseline: VGGT-only + DiffuEraser

To isolate the value of the SAM3 refinement step, we also consider an automatic baseline that bypasses SAM3 entirely, feeding raw VGGT-derived masks \(\{\tilde{M}_t\}\) (aligned to every frame, with temporal padding when the motion prior comes from a shorter window) directly to DiffuEraser prior to inpainting. While fully automatic, this VGGT-only variant is often ill-suited for strict single-object removal compared to the full pipeline.

Quantitative comparison

Data: batch evaluation on 2026-05-15 across 8 DAVIS sequences. vggt4d_xmem_sam3_vote_diffueraser (Ours) uses DiffuEraser for inpainting and achieves the best boundary precision (\(F_m\), \(F_r\)) and overall mask score. Mask metrics (\(J_m\), \(J_r\), mask score) are independent of the inpainter. Temporal warp is measured on inpainted frames.

Mask metrics (definitions, aligned with eval_stage.py)

Let \(P_t\) and \(G_t\) denote the predicted and ground-truth binary foreground masks at frame \(t\) (after alignment and optional object merging). Write \(\mathrm{IoU}_t = \lvert P_t \cap G_t \rvert \,/\, \lvert P_t \cup G_t \rvert\) (with the convention \(\mathrm{IoU}_t = 1\) if both masks are empty).

\(J_m\) (region similarity, mask_jm): per-frame IoU averaged over the sequence, \[ J_m \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathrm{IoU}_t . \] \(J_r\) (region “recall” style, mask_jr): fraction of frames with \(\mathrm{IoU}_t > 0.5\), \[ J_r \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbf{1}\bigl[\mathrm{IoU}_t > 0.5\bigr], \] where \(\mathbf{1}[\cdot]\) is the indicator function (1 if true, 0 otherwise).

\(F_m\) (mask_fm) and \(F_r\) (mask_fr) summarize boundary agreement: for each frame, prediction and GT boundaries are obtained from morphological gradients, optionally dilated into matching bands; precision and recall are computed on those bands, then an \(F_1\) score. \(F_m\) is the mean of per-frame boundary \(F_1\); \(F_r\) is the mean of per-frame boundary recall (see implementation in _boundary_f_pr).

mask_score averages the four components:

\[ ext{mask_score} = \dfrac{J_m + J_r + F_m + F_r}{4} \]

The table below reports \(J_m\), \(J_r\), mask_score, and temporal warp (temporal_warp_error_mean): a lower warp value indicates lower average optical-flow warping error (see evaluation pipeline for masking of hole vs background).

Why we do not emphasize PSNR / SSIM for inpainting here

PSNR and SSIM compare predicted pixels to a single reference image (or video frame-by-frame). For object removal + inpainting, a faithful result is not unique: many distinct inpainted backgrounds are perceptually acceptable and semantically correct, yet differ in every pixel from any one “GT” completion.

When no inpainted ground-truth video exists (or when only the original clip is available so the object is still present in the reference), pixel-wise metrics either cannot be defined sensibly, or they penalize valid solutions that disagree with an arbitrary reference. They also conflate mask errors (wrong hole shape) with texture choices inside a correct hole. For these reasons, this page highlights mask-based and temporal-consistency-oriented summaries rather than claiming PSNR/SSIM faithfully rank inpainting quality without a proper inpainting GT.
